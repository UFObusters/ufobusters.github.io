    Video Killed the UFO Star

    How has technology not killed the UFO phenomena yet?

    By Martine de Vos, Davide Ceolin, Anca Dumitrache, Albert Merono and Benjamin Timmermans

    On the VU SemWeb Outing - With all due respect to the fantastic team behind the special effects of the movie Independence Day (Roland Emmerich, 1996), how can we possibly see them under a realist prism today? When we enter a theater, we prepare ourselves for believing the story that we are about to see. We are willing to believe. What is the real power of this willing to believe, and how is it related to technology, manipulation, and human intentions?

    The long belief in flying saucers is the "real world" example in which this intersection occurs. It is more worrying so that, in this case, it happens out of the theater.

    The Cold Hard Facts

    Over 80 000 UFO reports have been registered all over the world. However, the rise of social media can mean the end of the UFO era.

    Difference between sourced and unsourced UFO sightings. -- Wikipedia and https://github.com/planetsig/ufo-reports

    The era of data and the Web has not impeded the proliferation of reports on UFO sightings. All the contrary. Statements about incredible sightings are all over the Web. "The object was a brilliant white light standing out against the bright Arizona sky"

    Humans are naive and credulous by nature. They have always been. First it was chamans; then alchemists; then UFOs. Believing in the unknown is something intrinsic to humans. Nowadays, humans believe in fake news.

    Recent technology developments, and the raise of accessible and widespread electronic devices, has changed this evidence supply mechanism. Most of these devices can record an comparatively high number of high resolution photographs and videos. In practice, this means that citizens from all social classes and incomes use these devices in their everyday lives. According to the popularity of the UFO phenomenon in past decades, one could have expected this technology spread make it easier to provide reliable evidence on the existence of UFOs.

    "You have to be willing to see" is a persistent quote of special FBI agent Fox Mulder in the popular TV series from the 90s The X-Files. Besides spreading information faster, technology can also magnify the willing of people to believe in insubstantiated facts. -- FOX

    Nevertheless, no such high resolution, multiple witness evidence has emerged as definitive proof of the existence of flying saucers. Today we have 4k recording devices in our pockets. So the UFO thing is not a thing anymore.

    People have switched from UFOs to fake news.

    Even if certifiable fake statements on vaccination and rosevelt atopsy are debunked by its very authors.

    Believers want to believe. No evidence or debunk will ever convince them.

    The solution proves not trivial so far, but we devise two measures that could work as potential solutions to the problem: humor for the short term, and education for the long term.

    Humor, ridiculization, and making charlatans out of the conversation could be a powerful humoristic way of countering the effects of fake news proliferators. The main advantage of these people is that they are way faster at creating the fake news than people debunking them scientifically.

    (sort term solution) Ridiculizing, making them out of the conversation. There is no way to keep up on their pace (issuing fake news is faster than benuking them)

    (long term solution) Education, in particular in how technology works.

    Number of UFO sighting reports, versus average resolution of cameras in different technological periods. Powerful high definition cameras in the pockets of common people do not seem to have an influence in the number of reported events; but have certainly changed the way in which photographic evidence is (not) provided. -- Wikipedia and https://github.com/planetsig/ufo-reports

    Number of sight reports versus number of users of the social network Facebook (in millions) over time. We can observe how technology empowers the spread and proliferation of all kinds of information, regardless of their quality or accompanying provenance. There is a significant boost of UFO sight reports since Facebook opened in 2004, and the absolute numbers of sight reports per year are higher than ever. -- Wikipedia, https://github.com/planetsig/ufo-reports, and The Guardian
